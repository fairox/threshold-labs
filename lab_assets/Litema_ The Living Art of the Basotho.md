# Litema: The Living Art of the Basotho

*A Coffee Table Book*

By Mokhethi Rampeta

## Foreword

In the rolling highlands of Lesotho and the vast plains of the Free State, where the earth meets the sky in an endless dance of colour and form, there exists an art form that speaks in the language of geometry and dreams in the palette of the earth itself. Litema, the traditional mural art of the Basotho people, represents far more than mere decoration—it is a living testament to the enduring power of women's creativity, the deep connection between human beings and their environment, and the remarkable ability of cultural traditions to adapt and survive across centuries of change.

This book emerges from a profound respect for the Basotho communities who have maintained this extraordinary tradition through generations of challenge and transformation. It is not merely an academic exercise or an aesthetic appreciation, but rather an attempt to honor the knowledge, skill, and cultural wisdom of the women who have kept Litema alive through their hands, their hearts, and their unwavering commitment to beauty in the midst of daily life.

The geometric patterns that grace the walls of Basotho homes are prayers made visible, stories told in earth and ochre, and connections to ancestors that transcend the boundaries between the physical and spiritual worlds. Each line carved into wet clay, each colour applied with careful intention, represents a continuation of knowledge that stretches back through centuries, linking contemporary practitioners to their foremothers in an unbroken chain of artistic and cultural transmission.

As we face an era of rapid globalization and cultural homogenization, the preservation and celebration of traditions like Litema becomes not just important but essential. These art forms remind us of the diversity of human expression, the beauty that emerges from deep cultural roots, and the wisdom that can be found in practices that have sustained communities across generations. They challenge us to look beyond the dominant narratives of art history and to recognize the sophisticated aesthetic and symbolic systems that have flourished in every corner of our world.

This book invites you on a journey through the world of Litema—from its ancient origins to its contemporary revival, from the intimate process of creation to the broader cultural meanings that give it life. It is a journey that requires us to slow down, to look carefully, and to listen respectfully to voices that have too often been marginalized in discussions of art and culture. It asks us to appreciate not just the visual beauty of these geometric patterns, but the complex social, spiritual, and environmental relationships that they represent.

The women who create Litema are not just artists in the Western sense of the term—they are cultural bearers, spiritual practitioners, community builders, and keepers of ancient wisdom. Their work connects the domestic sphere to the cosmic order, the individual home to the broader landscape, and the present moment to the eternal cycles of seasons, generations, and spiritual renewal. Through their hands, mud and dung become vehicles for beauty, meaning, and cultural continuity.

In presenting this work to an international audience, we are acutely aware of the responsibilities that come with cultural translation and representation. This book has been created in partnership with Basotho communities, with their guidance, approval, and ongoing involvement. It seeks to honor their ownership of this cultural knowledge while sharing its beauty and significance with a broader world that has much to learn from the wisdom embedded in these ancient practices.

May this book serve not only as a celebration of Litema's extraordinary beauty but as a bridge between cultures, a source of inspiration for artists and cultural workers everywhere, and a contribution to the vital work of preserving and honoring the world's diverse cultural heritage. Most importantly, may it serve the Basotho communities themselves as a record of their remarkable artistic legacy and a tool for ensuring that this living tradition continues to flourish for generations to come.

# Chapter 1: The Language of Earth

*[Image: Double-page spread showing a panoramic view of a Basotho village nestled in the mountains, with several houses displaying vibrant Litema patterns on their walls, the geometric designs creating a striking contrast against the natural landscape]*

In the early morning light of the Maloti Mountains, as mist rises from the valleys and the first rays of sun touch the peaks that give Lesotho its name as the "Kingdom in the Sky," something extraordinary becomes visible across the landscape. Scattered throughout the villages that dot these highlands, the walls of traditional homes come alive with intricate geometric patterns that seem to pulse with their own inner rhythm. These are not mere decorations, but expressions of a sophisticated artistic tradition that has transformed the humble materials of earth and dung into vehicles for beauty, meaning, and cultural continuity.

This is Litema—pronounced "di-te-ma" in the musical cadences of Sesotho—an art form that embodies the profound connection between the Basotho people and their environment, between women and their creative power, and between the practical necessities of daily life and the human need for beauty and meaning. To understand Litema is to enter into a world where art and life are inseparable, where the walls of a home become canvases for expressing everything from agricultural cycles to spiritual beliefs, from personal identity to community belonging.

The word itself carries layers of meaning that reveal the deep cultural significance of this practice. Derived from the verb "ho lema," meaning "to cultivate" or "to plough," Litema connects the act of artistic creation directly to the fundamental activity that sustains life in these agricultural communities. Just as the earth is cultivated to produce crops that nourish the body, the walls of homes are cultivated with patterns that nourish the spirit and strengthen the bonds that hold communities together.

This connection between cultivation and creation is not merely metaphorical but reflects a profound understanding of the relationship between human beings and their environment. The same hands that work the fields, that plant and tend and harvest, are the hands that mix the clay and dung, that carve the patterns and apply the colours that transform a simple mud wall into a work of art. The same seasonal rhythms that govern agricultural life also govern the creation of Litema, with new patterns often applied during times of celebration, renewal, and community gathering.

*[Image: Close-up of weathered hands mixing clay and dung, showing the tactile nature of the medium and the skill required to achieve the right consistency]*

The creation of Litema is fundamentally a women's art, though this designation carries none of the diminishment that such categorization might imply in other contexts. In Basotho culture, women's roles as creators and nurturers extend far beyond the domestic sphere to encompass responsibilities for cultural transmission, spiritual practice, and community cohesion. The women who create Litema are recognized as artists, cultural bearers, and spiritual practitioners whose work connects the individual household to the broader cosmos.

This gendered aspect of Litema reflects deeper cultural understandings about the relationship between women and the earth, between feminine creativity and the cycles of fertility and renewal that govern both agricultural and human life. The patterns that women create on their walls are often understood as prayers for fertility, abundance, and protection—appeals to ancestors and spiritual forces that are believed to influence the well-being of families and communities.

The materials used in Litema creation speak to this intimate connection with the earth and its cycles. The base layer consists of mud mixed with cow dung, a combination that might seem humble to outside observers but represents sophisticated knowledge about building techniques and material properties. The dung serves not merely as a binder but as a source of strength and durability, its high ammonia content acting as a natural antiseptic that helps preserve the mud surface and creates a foundation that can last for months or even years with proper care.

*[Image: A woman applying the base layer of mud and dung to a wall, showing the smooth, practiced motions that create an even surface]*

Over this foundation, the geometric patterns are created through a variety of techniques that demonstrate remarkable technical skill and artistic sophistication. Some patterns are carved or incised into the wet surface using fingers, sticks, or specially crafted tools, creating textural variations that catch and play with light throughout the day. Others are created through the application of different coloured clays and ochres, building up layers of meaning through colour symbolism that connects to spiritual beliefs and cultural values.

The colours themselves carry profound significance within Basotho culture. Red ochre, known as "letsoku," is understood as the "blood of the earth" and is associated with fertility, menstruation, and the life force that flows through all living things. White clay represents purity, calm, and the enlightenment that comes from connection with ancestors. Black, often created through the use of charcoal or specific clays, symbolizes the dark rain clouds that bring life-giving water and represents the power of ancestors to influence the natural world.

These colours are not applied randomly but according to sophisticated systems of meaning that vary between regions, families, and individual artists. The same pattern might carry different meanings depending on its colour, its placement on the wall, and its relationship to other patterns in the overall composition. This complexity reflects the depth of cultural knowledge required to create and interpret Litema, knowledge that is passed down through generations of women who serve as both artists and cultural teachers.

*[Image: A detailed view of a Litema pattern showing the interplay of different colours and textures, with the geometric precision clearly visible]*

The geometric nature of Litema patterns reflects not only aesthetic preferences but also deeper cultural understandings about the structure of reality itself. The repetitive, symmetrical designs that characterize most Litema work are understood to mirror the patterns that govern natural phenomena—the regular cycles of seasons, the mathematical relationships that underlie plant growth, the geometric structures that can be observed in everything from spider webs to crystal formations.

This geometric sensibility connects Basotho artistic traditions to broader African aesthetic systems that emphasize pattern, rhythm, and mathematical relationships as fundamental principles of beauty and meaning. The patterns are not merely decorative but are understood to participate in the cosmic order, to create harmony between human habitation and natural forces, and to establish connections between the visible world and invisible spiritual realities.

The creation of Litema is typically a communal activity that brings together women from extended families and neighborhoods in collaborative artistic endeavors. These gatherings serve multiple functions beyond the purely artistic, providing opportunities for social bonding, cultural transmission, and community problem-solving. Young girls learn by watching and gradually participating, absorbing not only the technical skills required to create the patterns but also the cultural knowledge and social relationships that give them meaning.

*[Image: A group of women working together on a large wall, some mixing materials, others applying patterns, with children watching and learning]*

The timing of Litema creation is closely tied to the rhythms of agricultural and social life. New patterns are often applied in preparation for special occasions—weddings, religious ceremonies, seasonal celebrations, or the arrival of important visitors. The act of creating Litema thus becomes a way of marking significant moments in community life, of preparing spaces for celebration and gathering, and of demonstrating respect for guests and ancestors alike.

This temporal aspect of Litema reflects its fundamentally ephemeral nature. Unlike art forms that are designed to last for centuries, Litema is created with the understanding that it will fade, crack, and eventually be washed away by rain or worn away by wind and sun. This impermanence is not seen as a flaw but as an essential characteristic that connects the art form to the cycles of renewal that govern all life. Just as crops are planted, grown, harvested, and planted again, Litema patterns are created, enjoyed, allowed to fade, and created anew.

This cycle of creation and renewal means that Litema is always contemporary, always responsive to current conditions and concerns. While traditional patterns and techniques are preserved and transmitted across generations, each new application provides opportunities for innovation, adaptation, and personal expression. Contemporary Litema artists work within established traditions while also responding to changing materials, new influences, and evolving cultural contexts.

*[Image: A wall showing Litema in various stages of weathering, from fresh and vibrant patterns to older, faded designs, illustrating the temporal nature of the art form]*

The patterns themselves draw inspiration from multiple sources that reflect the full range of Basotho experience and observation. Many designs are based on agricultural imagery—the furrows of ploughed fields, the patterns of planted crops, the textures of harvested grain. Others derive from natural phenomena such as water flowing over rocks, the geometric structures of plants and flowers, or the movement of clouds across mountain peaks.

Daily life provides another rich source of pattern inspiration. The geometric designs used in traditional crafts such as basket weaving and pottery making find their way onto walls, as do patterns derived from clothing, blankets, and other textiles. Even modern influences such as magazine illustrations, architectural details, and commercial products can be absorbed and transformed through the geometric language of Litema.

This openness to diverse influences reflects the dynamic nature of Basotho culture and its capacity for adaptation and innovation while maintaining core values and practices. Litema serves as a visual record of cultural change, incorporating new elements while preserving traditional knowledge and techniques. In this way, the walls of Basotho homes become archives of cultural history, recording the evolution of aesthetic preferences, the introduction of new materials and techniques, and the ongoing dialogue between tradition and modernity.

The social dimensions of Litema extend beyond the immediate community to encompass relationships with the broader landscape and spiritual world. The patterns are understood to create connections between the domestic space of the home and the wider environment, establishing harmony between human habitation and natural forces. They also serve as a form of communication with ancestors, whose presence is believed to be particularly strong in and around the home.

*[Image: A traditional Basotho home with Litema patterns, set against the dramatic landscape of the Maloti Mountains, showing the integration of human creativity with natural beauty]*

This spiritual dimension of Litema reflects broader Basotho beliefs about the relationship between the living and the dead, between the visible and invisible worlds. The patterns are often understood as prayers made visible, as appeals for protection, fertility, and abundance that are directed toward ancestral spirits who are believed to have the power to influence the well-being of their descendants.

The geometric nature of these spiritual communications reflects sophisticated understandings about the nature of spiritual reality and the ways in which human beings can establish and maintain connections with forces beyond the material world. The repetitive, symmetrical patterns are believed to create resonances that can be perceived by spiritual beings, establishing channels of communication that transcend the boundaries between different levels of reality.

As we stand before a wall decorated with Litema patterns, we are witnessing far more than an aesthetic achievement, remarkable though that achievement may be. We are seeing the visible expression of a complex cultural system that encompasses agricultural knowledge, spiritual beliefs, social relationships, and artistic traditions. We are observing the work of artists who understand their practice as simultaneously practical and sacred, individual and communal, traditional and innovative.

The language of earth that speaks through Litema is a language of profound sophistication and beauty, one that has evolved over centuries to express the full range of human experience and aspiration. It is a language that connects the most practical aspects of daily life to the most transcendent spiritual realities, that finds beauty in humble materials and meaning in geometric relationships, that celebrates both individual creativity and collective wisdom.

In the chapters that follow, we will explore the rich history of this remarkable art form, delve deeper into its symbolic meanings and cultural significance, meet the contemporary artists who are keeping the tradition alive, and consider the challenges and opportunities that face Litema in an era of rapid cultural change. Through this journey, we hope to develop a deeper appreciation not only for the visual beauty of these geometric patterns but for the complex cultural world that gives them life and meaning.

The story of Litema is ultimately a story about the human capacity for creating beauty and meaning in the midst of daily life, about the power of women's creativity to transform humble materials into vehicles for cultural expression, and about the remarkable ability of traditional knowledge to adapt and survive across generations of change. It is a story that speaks to universal human needs and aspirations while remaining deeply rooted in the specific cultural soil from which it has grown.

As the morning mist clears from the Maloti Mountains and the geometric patterns on village walls emerge in sharp relief against the African sky, we are reminded that art is not a luxury but a necessity, not an ornament but an essential expression of what it means to be human. In the language of earth that speaks through Litema, we hear echoes of our own deepest needs for beauty, meaning, and connection—needs that transcend cultural boundaries while finding their most profound expression in the particular traditions that have sustained communities across the centuries.

# Chapter 2: Roots in the Earth

*[Image: Double-page spread showing a historical photograph from Gary van Wyk's collection, featuring a traditional Basotho homestead from the early 20th century with clearly visible Litema patterns, set against the timeless landscape of the Free State]*

The story of Litema stretches back through centuries of Basotho history, its roots extending deep into the cultural soil of Southern Africa long before the first European observers arrived to document what they saw. Like the geometric patterns themselves, this history unfolds in layers, each period adding new elements while preserving essential characteristics that connect contemporary practitioners to their ancestors in an unbroken chain of artistic and cultural transmission.

To trace the origins of Litema is to embark on a journey through the complex history of the Basotho people themselves—a journey that encompasses migration and settlement, conflict and adaptation, cultural preservation and innovation. It is a story that reveals how art forms can serve as repositories of cultural memory, preserving knowledge and values across generations of change while adapting to new circumstances and influences.

The earliest roots of Litema can be traced to the broader traditions of mural art that have flourished across Southern and Eastern Africa for millennia. Archaeological evidence suggests that the practice of decorating walls with geometric patterns has ancient origins in this region, with examples found in rock shelters and archaeological sites that date back hundreds and possibly thousands of years. These early examples share many characteristics with contemporary Litema—the use of earth-based pigments, the emphasis on geometric patterns, and the integration of artistic practice with daily life and spiritual beliefs.

*[Image: Archaeological evidence showing ancient geometric patterns on rock surfaces, demonstrating the deep historical roots of Southern African mural traditions]*

The specific traditions that would eventually become known as Litema likely developed as part of the broader cultural complex associated with Sotho-Tswana speaking peoples who settled in the region that is now Lesotho and the Free State province of South Africa. These communities brought with them sophisticated knowledge of agriculture, metallurgy, and artistic production, including traditions of pottery decoration, textile production, and architectural ornamentation that would provide the foundation for later developments in mural art.

The mountainous terrain of what would become Lesotho provided both opportunities and challenges for these early settlers. The high altitude and dramatic seasonal variations required adaptations in agricultural techniques, building methods, and social organization that would profoundly influence the development of cultural practices including Litema. The abundance of different coloured clays and ochres in the mountain environment provided a rich palette of materials for artistic expression, while the harsh climate necessitated building techniques that emphasized durability and weather resistance.

The social organization of early Basotho communities also played a crucial role in shaping the development of Litema traditions. The emphasis on extended family networks, communal labor, and collective decision-making created contexts in which artistic practices could flourish as expressions of group identity and social cohesion. The important roles played by women in agricultural production, household management, and cultural transmission provided the social foundation for the development of Litema as a predominantly feminine art form.

*[Image: A traditional Basotho village layout showing the relationship between individual homesteads and the broader community structure that supports Litema practice]*

The first documented European observations of Litema date to the early nineteenth century, when missionaries and travelers began to record their encounters with Basotho communities. These early accounts, while often filtered through European cultural assumptions and biases, provide valuable insights into the state of Litema practice during a period of significant cultural and political change.

The Reverend John Campbell, who traveled through the region in 1813 and again in 1820, was among the first Europeans to document the practice of wall decoration among Basotho communities. His accounts describe the geometric patterns he observed with a mixture of admiration and puzzlement, recognizing their aesthetic appeal while struggling to understand their cultural significance. Campbell's observations are particularly valuable because they predate many of the major disruptions that would later affect Basotho society, providing a glimpse of Litema practice during a period of relative cultural stability.

Eugene Casallis, a French missionary who worked among the Basotho from 1833 to 1855, provided more detailed and culturally sensitive observations of Litema practice. His accounts describe not only the visual characteristics of the patterns but also their social and spiritual significance within Basotho culture. Casallis recognized that the geometric designs were not merely decorative but served important functions in marking social status, expressing religious beliefs, and maintaining connections between communities and their ancestral heritage.

*[Image: Historical illustration or photograph from the missionary period showing Basotho homes with Litema patterns, demonstrating the continuity of the tradition across time]*

The period of the mid-nineteenth century brought significant challenges to Basotho society that would profoundly affect the development of Litema traditions. The arrival of European settlers, the establishment of colonial administrations, and the disruptions caused by conflicts such as the Lifaqane (a period of widespread warfare and displacement) created pressures that threatened traditional cultural practices while also creating new contexts for cultural adaptation and innovation.

During this period, Litema practice demonstrated remarkable resilience and adaptability. While some traditional patterns and techniques were lost or modified, new influences were absorbed and transformed through the geometric language of Basotho artistic tradition. European architectural elements, new materials and tools, and changing social circumstances all contributed to the evolution of Litema while its essential characteristics and cultural functions remained intact.

The establishment of Basutoland (later Lesotho) as a British protectorate in 1868 created new political and economic conditions that would influence the development of Litema in complex ways. On one hand, the protectorate status provided some protection for traditional cultural practices by limiting the extent of European settlement and cultural interference. On the other hand, the introduction of new economic systems, educational institutions, and religious practices created pressures for cultural change that affected all aspects of Basotho life.

*[Image: A timeline visualization showing key historical periods and their influence on Litema development, with examples of patterns from different eras]*

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries saw significant documentation efforts by anthropologists, government officials, and other observers who recognized the cultural importance of Litema and sought to record its characteristics before they might be lost to modernization. These documentation efforts, while sometimes problematic in their assumptions and methods, provide valuable records of Litema practice during a period of rapid change.

George Stow, whose "Native Races of South Africa" was published in 1905, included detailed descriptions and illustrations of Basotho wall decorations that demonstrate both the continuity of traditional patterns and the innovations that were occurring in response to changing circumstances. Stow's work is particularly valuable for its attention to regional variations in Litema practice and its recognition of the sophisticated artistic and technical knowledge required for its creation.

The early twentieth century brought new challenges and opportunities for Litema practice. The introduction of new building materials and techniques, the growth of urban centers, and the increasing integration of Basotho communities into regional and global economic systems all created pressures for change while also providing new contexts for cultural expression and adaptation.

During this period, some observers predicted the imminent disappearance of traditional practices like Litema, assuming that modernization would inevitably lead to cultural homogenization and the abandonment of indigenous artistic traditions. However, these predictions proved to be overly pessimistic, as Litema demonstrated remarkable capacity for adaptation and survival even under conditions of rapid social and economic change.

*[Image: Comparison showing Litema patterns from the early 20th century alongside contemporary examples, illustrating both continuity and change in the tradition]*

The mid-twentieth century brought new forms of documentation and scholarly attention to Litema that would prove crucial for its preservation and revival. The work of researchers like Gary van Wyk, who conducted extensive field research in the 1990s, provided comprehensive documentation of Litema practice at a time when many traditional knowledge holders were still active and accessible for interviews and collaboration.

Van Wyk's research, which resulted in the landmark publication "African Painted Houses" in 1998, represented a new approach to documenting Litema that emphasized collaboration with community members, respect for cultural ownership, and attention to the voices and perspectives of the women who created and maintained the tradition. His work provided not only visual documentation of remarkable quality but also detailed information about the cultural meanings, technical processes, and social contexts that give Litema its significance.

The documentation efforts of the late twentieth century revealed both the resilience of Litema traditions and the challenges they faced in an era of rapid modernization. While many traditional patterns and techniques had been preserved, researchers also found evidence of significant losses in cultural knowledge, particularly among younger generations who had less exposure to traditional practices and less opportunity to learn from elder knowledge holders.

These findings sparked new interest in preservation and revival efforts that would characterize the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries. Cultural organizations, educational institutions, and government agencies began to recognize Litema as an important part of Basotho cultural heritage that deserved protection and support. These efforts took various forms, from documentation projects and educational programs to exhibitions and cultural festivals that celebrated Litema and other traditional arts.

*[Image: A contemporary cultural festival or workshop showing people learning Litema techniques, demonstrating the ongoing efforts to preserve and transmit the tradition]*

The historical development of Litema reveals several important patterns that help us understand both its significance and its contemporary challenges. First, the tradition has demonstrated remarkable resilience and adaptability, surviving centuries of political, economic, and social change while maintaining its essential characteristics and cultural functions. This resilience reflects not only the strength of Basotho cultural institutions but also the inherent flexibility and adaptability of the Litema tradition itself.

Second, the history of Litema shows how artistic traditions can serve as repositories of cultural memory, preserving knowledge and values across generations while also adapting to new circumstances and influences. The geometric patterns that characterize Litema have served as vehicles for transmitting everything from technical knowledge about building and agriculture to spiritual beliefs about ancestors and the natural world.

Third, the documentation of Litema history reveals the importance of collaborative and culturally sensitive approaches to cultural preservation. The most successful efforts to document and preserve Litema have been those that involved community members as partners rather than subjects, that respected cultural ownership and authority, and that sought to serve community needs and priorities rather than purely academic or commercial interests.

The story of Litema's historical development also highlights the complex relationships between tradition and modernity, between cultural preservation and adaptation. Rather than representing a simple opposition between old and new, the history of Litema shows how traditional practices can evolve and adapt while maintaining their essential characteristics and cultural significance. This understanding has important implications for contemporary efforts to support and sustain Litema practice in an era of continued rapid change.

*[Image: A master practitioner teaching a young woman Litema techniques, symbolizing the intergenerational transmission that has sustained the tradition throughout its history]*

As we trace the roots of Litema through the layers of Basotho history, we see a tradition that has grown and evolved like a living organism, drawing nourishment from its cultural environment while adapting to changing conditions and circumstances. The geometric patterns that grace the walls of Basotho homes today carry within them the accumulated wisdom of centuries, the creative innovations of countless individual artists, and the collective memory of a people who have maintained their cultural identity through periods of profound challenge and change.

The historical journey of Litema reminds us that cultural traditions are not static artifacts from the past but living, breathing expressions of human creativity and cultural continuity. They survive not by remaining unchanged but by finding ways to preserve their essential characteristics while adapting to new circumstances and opportunities. In this sense, the history of Litema provides a model for understanding how traditional knowledge and practices can remain relevant and vital in contemporary contexts.

The roots of Litema run deep in the earth of Basotho culture, drawing strength from ancient sources while continuing to grow and flourish in response to contemporary conditions. As we move forward to explore the symbolic meanings and contemporary expressions of this remarkable tradition, we carry with us an appreciation for the long historical journey that has brought Litema to its current state and the countless individuals who have contributed to its preservation and development across the generations.

The story of Litema's historical development is ultimately a story about the power of human creativity to transcend the limitations of time and circumstance, to preserve what is most valuable from the past while remaining open to new possibilities and innovations. It is a story that speaks to the resilience of cultural traditions and the importance of maintaining connections to ancestral wisdom while embracing the opportunities and challenges of contemporary life.

# Chapter 3: Sacred Geometry

*[Image: Double-page spread showing a detailed analysis of Litema geometric patterns, with close-up photographs revealing the mathematical precision and symbolic complexity of the designs]*

In the intricate geometric patterns that flow across the walls of Basotho homes lies a sophisticated language of meaning that speaks to the deepest aspects of human experience—our relationship with the natural world, our connections to spiritual forces, and our understanding of the mathematical principles that govern the universe itself. To learn to read this language is to enter into a realm where art and mathematics, spirituality and daily life, individual expression and collective wisdom converge in patterns of extraordinary beauty and profound significance.

The geometric nature of Litema is not accidental but reflects fundamental Basotho understandings about the structure of reality and the principles that govern both natural phenomena and human society. The repetitive, symmetrical patterns that characterize most Litema work are understood to mirror the mathematical relationships that can be observed throughout the natural world—from the spiral arrangements of seeds in a sunflower to the hexagonal structures of honeycomb, from the branching patterns of rivers to the crystalline formations of minerals.

This mathematical sensibility connects Basotho artistic traditions to broader African aesthetic systems that emphasize pattern, rhythm, and geometric relationships as fundamental principles of beauty and meaning. The patterns are not merely decorative but are understood to participate in the cosmic order, to create harmony between human habitation and natural forces, and to establish connections between the visible world and invisible spiritual realities.

*[Image: A grid showing common Litema patterns with their traditional names in Sesotho and English translations, demonstrating the diversity and complexity of the geometric vocabulary]*

The symbolic meanings embedded in Litema patterns operate on multiple levels simultaneously, creating layers of significance that can be read and interpreted according to different contexts and purposes. At the most basic level, many patterns derive their meanings from direct visual resemblance to objects, phenomena, or activities from daily life. The zigzag lines that appear in many designs, for example, are often understood to represent water flowing over rocks, the furrows of ploughed fields, or the lightning that accompanies thunderstorms.

These literal interpretations, however, represent only the surface layer of a much more complex symbolic system. The same zigzag pattern that represents water at one level might also symbolize the life force that flows through all living things, the spiritual energy that connects human beings to their ancestors, or the dynamic tension between opposing forces that drives the cycles of growth and renewal. This multiplicity of meanings allows Litema patterns to function as vehicles for expressing complex philosophical and spiritual concepts that might be difficult to communicate through words alone.

The colours used in Litema carry their own rich symbolic associations that interact with the geometric patterns to create additional layers of meaning. Red ochre, the most important and widely used pigment in traditional Litema, is understood as the "blood of the earth" and carries associations with fertility, life force, and the sacred power of women. When applied to patterns that represent agricultural activities, red ochre transforms them into prayers for abundant harvests and fertile fields. When used in patterns associated with human figures or domestic activities, it invokes the life-giving power of women and their central role in maintaining family and community well-being.

*[Image: Close-up photographs showing the application of different coloured pigments, with detailed views of how colour interacts with pattern to create meaning]*

White clay, another fundamental colour in the Litema palette, represents purity, peace, and the enlightenment that comes from connection with ancestral spirits. White patterns are often associated with spiritual protection, ritual purification, and the wisdom of elders. The use of white in Litema designs can transform geometric patterns into visual prayers for spiritual guidance, protection from harm, and maintenance of harmony between the living and the dead.

Black, created through the use of charcoal or specific dark clays, symbolizes the fertile darkness of the earth, the rain clouds that bring life-giving water, and the mysterious power of ancestors to influence events in the world of the living. Black patterns often appear in designs associated with agricultural fertility, spiritual power, and the cyclical nature of life and death. The strategic use of black in Litema compositions can create dramatic visual effects while invoking the protective and generative powers of ancestral spirits.

The symbolic system of Litema extends beyond individual patterns and colours to encompass the overall composition and placement of designs on the wall surface. The organization of patterns into horizontal bands, vertical columns, or radiating arrangements creates additional layers of meaning that relate to cosmological concepts, social hierarchies, and spiritual geographies. The placement of certain patterns near doorways, windows, or other architectural features can invoke specific forms of protection or blessing for those who enter or leave the home.

*[Image: A complete wall showing how individual patterns combine into larger compositional arrangements, with annotations indicating the symbolic significance of different areas and relationships]*

Many Litema patterns derive their meanings from agricultural imagery, reflecting the central importance of farming in Basotho life and the deep connections between artistic expression and the cycles of planting, growing, and harvesting that sustain the community. Patterns representing ploughed fields, planted crops, and harvested grain appear frequently in Litema designs, often combined with symbols of rain, sun, and fertile earth to create comprehensive visual statements about agricultural abundance and community prosperity.

The "Morabaraba" pattern, named after a traditional board game, represents one of the most widespread and recognizable designs in the Litema repertoire. This pattern, consisting of intersecting lines that create a grid-like structure, carries multiple layers of meaning that extend far beyond its gaming associations. At one level, it represents the ordered structure of agricultural fields and the careful planning required for successful farming. At another level, it symbolizes the social networks and reciprocal relationships that bind communities together. At the deepest level, it represents the cosmic order itself—the invisible grid of relationships and forces that governs all existence.

Natural phenomena provide another rich source of symbolic content for Litema patterns. Designs representing mountains, rivers, clouds, and celestial bodies appear frequently in traditional compositions, often combined with agricultural and domestic imagery to create comprehensive visual statements about the relationship between human communities and their natural environment. These patterns serve not only as decorative elements but as visual reminders of the dependence of human life on natural forces and the importance of maintaining harmony with the environment.

*[Image: Detailed photographs of patterns inspired by natural phenomena, showing how abstract geometric designs can represent complex environmental relationships]*

The representation of water in Litema patterns deserves special attention, given the crucial importance of water in the semi-arid environment where many Basotho communities live. Patterns representing flowing water, standing pools, and falling rain appear in numerous variations throughout the Litema tradition, often combined with symbols of fertility and abundance to create powerful visual prayers for adequate rainfall and successful harvests. The geometric abstraction of these water patterns allows them to function simultaneously as realistic representations and spiritual symbols, bridging the gap between the physical and metaphysical aspects of water's significance.

Animal imagery, while less common than agricultural and natural patterns, plays an important role in certain Litema traditions. Stylized representations of cattle, which hold central importance in Basotho culture as symbols of wealth and status, appear in patterns that invoke prosperity and social standing. Birds, particularly those associated with rain and fertility, are represented in geometric forms that emphasize their spiritual significance rather than their physical appearance. These animal patterns often serve as clan or family symbols, connecting individual households to broader kinship networks and ancestral lineages.

The human figure, when it appears in Litema patterns, is typically rendered in highly stylized geometric forms that emphasize symbolic rather than realistic representation. These patterns often focus on activities central to women's roles in Basotho society—cooking, child-rearing, agricultural work, and ritual activities. The geometric abstraction of human figures allows them to function as universal symbols rather than portraits of specific individuals, representing the archetypal roles and relationships that structure social life.

# Chapter 4: Hands That Create

*[Image: Double-page spread showing a master Litema artist at work, her hands covered in clay as she carefully applies patterns to a freshly prepared wall, with the concentration and skill of generations evident in her movements]*

Her hands, weathered by decades of labor in fields and homes, move with practiced certainty as she gathers the materials she will need—clay from the riverbank, dung from the cattle kraal, water from the spring, and the precious ochre that will bring colour to her creation. Today, like countless days before, she will transform the humble wall of her home into a canvas for beauty, meaning, and cultural expression.

'Matsooana is one of the master practitioners who keep the tradition of Litema alive in contemporary Basotho society. The process of creating Litema begins long before the first pattern is applied to the wall. It starts with the careful selection and preparation of materials, a process that requires intimate knowledge of local resources and their properties. The clay must be of the right consistency and colour, free from impurities that might affect the durability or appearance of the finished work. The dung must be fresh enough to provide binding strength but aged enough to have lost its strong odor. The water must be clean and free from minerals that might discolour the mixture.

*[Image: Close-up of hands mixing clay and dung, showing the tactile knowledge required to achieve the proper consistency and texture]*

The preparation of the wall surface itself is a crucial step that determines the success of the entire project. The existing surface must be cleaned and, if necessary, repaired to provide a smooth, stable foundation for the new work. Any loose or damaged areas must be removed and replaced, and the entire surface must be dampened to ensure proper adhesion of the new materials. This preparatory work requires not only technical skill but also aesthetic judgment, as the artist must envision how the finished patterns will interact with the architectural features of the building.

The application of the base layer represents the first creative act in the Litema process. The mixture of clay and dung is applied with hands and simple tools, smoothed and shaped to create a uniform surface that will serve as the canvas for the geometric patterns to come. This seemingly simple step requires considerable skill and experience, as the thickness and texture of the base layer will affect every subsequent stage of the work. Too thick, and the surface may crack as it dries; too thin, and it may not provide adequate coverage or durability.

The creation of patterns begins while the base layer is still damp, allowing the artist to carve, incise, and shape the surface to create textural variations that will catch and reflect light throughout the day. Some patterns are created entirely through these textural techniques, using the play of light and shadow to define geometric relationships and create visual interest. Others serve as foundations for the application of coloured pigments that will add another dimension of meaning and beauty to the finished work.

*[Image: Sequential photographs showing the step-by-step process of pattern creation, from initial incisions to the application of coloured pigments]*

The tools used in Litema creation are typically simple implements that extend and refine the capabilities of the human hand. Fingers remain the primary tools for most work, their sensitivity and flexibility allowing for the precise control required to create clean lines and smooth curves. Sticks of various sizes and shapes serve as extensions of the fingers, allowing artists to reach into corners and create details that would be difficult to achieve with hands alone. Pieces of bone, metal, or carved wood may be used for specific purposes, such as creating particular textures or achieving certain types of lines.

The application of coloured pigments represents one of the most skilled and aesthetically demanding aspects of Litema creation. The artist must not only possess technical knowledge about the properties and behavior of different materials but also aesthetic judgment about colour relationships, symbolic meanings, and overall compositional balance. Each colour must be mixed to the proper consistency, applied at the right moment in the drying process, and integrated with other colours and patterns to create a harmonious and meaningful whole.

The timing of pigment application is crucial to the success of the work. Applied too early, while the base layer is still very wet, the colours may run or blur, destroying the crisp geometric relationships that characterize good Litema work. Applied too late, after the base has dried completely, the pigments may not adhere properly or may appear flat and lifeless. The experienced artist develops an intuitive sense of timing that allows her to work efficiently while maintaining the highest standards of quality and appearance.

*[Image: A master artist demonstrating the precise timing required for pigment application, showing how the consistency of the base layer affects the final result]*

The creation of complex Litema compositions often requires multiple working sessions spread over several days or even weeks. Each layer must be allowed to dry properly before the next can be applied, and weather conditions can significantly affect the timing and success of the work. Rain, excessive heat, or high winds can all interfere with the drying process and may require the artist to modify her plans or postpone certain stages of the work.

This temporal dimension of Litema creation connects the artistic process to the broader rhythms of daily life and seasonal change. # Glossary

**Basotho** - The people of Lesotho and related communities in South Africa; plural form (singular: Mosotho)

**Ditema** - Alternative spelling of Litema used in South African Sesotho orthography

**Ho lema** - Sesotho verb meaning "to cultivate" or "to plough," from which the word Litema is derived

**Letsoku** - Red ochre pigment used in traditional Litema, understood as the "blood of the earth"

**Litema** - Traditional Basotho mural art consisting of geometric patterns applied to walls using earth-based materials

**Maloti Mountains** - Mountain range in Lesotho, also known as the Drakensberg

**Morabaraba** - Traditional board game; also the name of a common Litema pattern based on the game board

**Mosotho** - A person of Basotho ethnicity; singular form (plural: Basotho)

**Ochre** - Natural earth pigment containing iron oxide, used for colouring in traditional art

**Sesotho** - The language of the Basotho people, also known as Southern Sotho

**Sotho-Tswana** - Language and cultural group including the Basotho and related peoples
